1.2 Multivariate Linear Model

In the standard linear model the responses \(y_i\) are univariate. In the multivariate linear model, the responses are \(p \times 1\) vectors \(\mathbf y_i\). Here, the linear model takes the form \[\begin{equation} \mathbf y_i=\mathbf B^\top \mathbf x_i +{\pmb \epsilon}_i, \qquad i=1, \ldots , n, \tag{1.6} \end{equation}\] where \(\stackrel{q \times p}{\mathbf B}\) is a parameter matrix, the \(\mathbf x_i\) are \(q \times 1\) covariate vectors as in 8.2, and the \({\pmb \epsilon}_i\) are \(p \times 1\) error vectors. The model (1.6) may be written in matrix form as \[\begin{equation} \mathbf Y= \mathbf X\mathbf B+\mathbf E, \tag{1.7} \end{equation}\] where \(\stackrel{n \times p}{\mathbf Y}=[\mathbf y_1, \ldots , \mathbf y_n]^\top\) is the data matrix for the \(y\)-variables, \(\mathbf X=[\mathbf x_1, \ldots , \mathbf x_n]^\top\) is the \(n \times q\) data matrix for the \(x\)-variables, defined as in 8.2, and \(\stackrel{n \times p}{\mathbf E}=[{\pmb \epsilon}_1, \ldots , {\pmb \epsilon}_n]^\top\).

By analogy with assumption 4. in 8.2, it is assumed that \[\begin{equation} {\pmb \epsilon_1}, \ldots , {\pmb \epsilon}_n \quad \text{are IID}\quad N_p({\mathbf 0}_p, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}). \tag{1.8} \end{equation}\]

Under this MVN assumption, the log-likelihood function \(\ell(\mathbf B, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})\) for the parameter matrices \(\mathbf B\) and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) is given by \[\begin{align} \ell(\mathbf B, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})&=-\frac{np}{2}\log(2\pi) -\frac{n}{2}\log(\vert \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\vert) \nonumber \\ & \qquad \qquad -\frac{1}{2}\text{tr}\left \{ (\mathbf Y-\mathbf X\mathbf B) \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf Y- \mathbf X\mathbf B)^\top\right \}. \tag{1.9} \end{align}\]

Proposition 1.1 The maximum likelihood estimators of \(\mathbf B\) and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) in (1.9) are given by \[\begin{equation} \hat{\mathbf B}= (\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf Y \tag{1.10} \end{equation}\] and \[\begin{equation} \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}=\frac{1}{n}\mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y, \tag{1.11} \end{equation}\] where \(\mathbf P\) is the matrix defined in (1.5). The maximised log-likelihood is given by \[\begin{equation} \ell(\hat{\mathbf B}, \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}})=- \frac{n}{2} \log(\vert \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}} \vert )-\frac{np}{2}\log(2\pi) -\frac{np}{2}. \tag{1.12} \end{equation}\]

Remark: note how similar (1.10) and (1.11) are to their univariate counterparts (1.3) and (1.4), respectively; the only thing that is different is that \(\mathbf Y\) is now an \(n \times p\) matrix rather than an \(n \times 1\) vector.

Proof. of (1.10)}. Recall that \(\mathbf P\) defined in (1.5) is a projection matrix, i.e. \(\mathbf P^\top =\mathbf P\) and \(\mathbf P^2 = \mathbf P\). Moreover, \[\begin{equation} \mathbf P\mathbf X=\mathbf X-\mathbf X(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X=\mathbf X-\mathbf X={\mathbf 0}_{n,q} \tag{1.13} \end{equation}\] and \[\begin{equation} \mathbf X^\top \mathbf P=\mathbf X^\top - \mathbf X^\top \mathbf X(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top =\mathbf X^\top -\mathbf X^\top ={\mathbf 0}_{q,n}. \tag{1.14} \end{equation}\] Now write \[\begin{align*} \mathbf Y-\mathbf X\mathbf B&=\mathbf Y-\mathbf X\hat{\mathbf B} +\mathbf X\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf X\mathbf B\\ &=\mathbf Y-\mathbf X(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf Y+\mathbf X(\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B)\\ &=\mathbf P\mathbf Y+\mathbf X(\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B). \end{align*}\] Then, using (1.13) and (1.14), \[\begin{align} (\mathbf Y&- \mathbf X\mathbf B)^\top (\mathbf Y- \mathbf X\mathbf B) \nonumber \\ &=\{\mathbf P\mathbf Y+ \mathbf X(\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B)\}^\top \{\mathbf P\mathbf Y+\mathbf X(\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B)\}\nonumber \\ &=\mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y+ \mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf X(\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B)\nonumber \\ & \qquad \qquad + (\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B)^\top \mathbf X^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y+(\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B)^\top \mathbf X^\top \mathbf X(\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B) \nonumber\\ &=\mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y+ (\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B)^\top \mathbf X^\top \mathbf X(\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B). \tag{1.15} \end{align}\] As noted in Chapter 2, for any compatible matrices \(\mathbf W\) and \(\mathbf Z\), we have \(\text{tr}(\mathbf W\mathbf Z)=\text{tr}(\mathbf Z\mathbf W)\). So, using (1.15), it follows that the trace term in (1.9) may be written \[\begin{align} &\text{tr}\left \{ (\mathbf Y-\mathbf X\mathbf B)\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf Y- \mathbf X\mathbf B)^\top \right \}\nonumber \\ &=\text{tr}\left \{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf Y-\mathbf X\mathbf B)^\top (\mathbf Y- \mathbf X\mathbf B) \right \}\nonumber \\ &=\text{tr}\left \{ \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} ( \mathbf Y-\mathbf X\hat{\mathbf B}+\mathbf X\hat{\mathbf B} -\mathbf X\mathbf B)^\top ( \mathbf Y-\mathbf X\hat{\mathbf B}+\mathbf X\hat{\mathbf B} -\mathbf X\mathbf B) \right \}\nonumber \\ &= \text{tr}\left [\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \{\mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y+(\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B)^\top \mathbf X^\top \mathbf X(\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B) \}\right ] \nonumber\\ &=\text{tr} \left (\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y\right ) + \text{tr}\left \{ \mathbf X(\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B) \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\hat{\mathbf B}-\mathbf B)^\top \mathbf X^\top \right \} \tag{1.16} \end{align}\] The first term on the RHS of (1.16) does not depend on \(\mathbf B\). In the second term, the matrix inside the trace is non-negative definite for all \(\mathbf B\), and therefore the second term in (1.16) is non-negative, and has a minimum value of \(0\), achieved uniquely when \(\mathbf B=\hat{\mathbf B}\). Therefore, for fixed \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\), (1.9) is maximised when \(\mathbf B=\hat{\mathbf B}\), as defined in (1.10). \(\square\)

To prove (1.11), we require the following result, whose proof is omitted because it is a bit too technical.

Proof. Suppose that \(\mathbf A\) is a fixed symmetric positive definite \(p \times p\) matrix and define the function \[ G(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}) \equiv -\frac{n}{2} \log \left (\vert \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\vert\right) -\frac{1}{2}\text{tr} \left (\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \mathbf A\right). \] Then \(G(\boldsymbol{\Sigma})\) is maximised over symmetric positive definite \(p \times p\) matrices \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) at \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}=n^{-1}\mathbf A\), and the maximum value of \(G\) is given by \[ G(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}})= -\frac{n}{2} \log \left (\vert n^{-1} \mathbf A\vert \right ) -np/2. \]

Proof. of (1.11) and (1.12)}. Again using the result \(\text{tr}(\mathbf W\mathbf Z)=\text{tr}(\mathbf Z\mathbf W)\) for compatible matrices \(\mathbf W\) and \(\mathbf Z\), we have \[ \text{tr}\{(\mathbf Y-\mathbf X\hat{\mathbf B}) \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\mathbf Y-\mathbf X\hat{\mathbf B})^\top\}=\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\mathbf P\mathbf Y)^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y)=\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y), \] because \(\mathbf P\) is a projection matrix. Consequently, \[ \ell(\hat{\mathbf B},\boldsymbol{\Sigma})=-\frac{np}{2}\log(2\pi)-\frac{n}{2}\log \vert \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\vert -\frac{1}{2} \text{tr} \left \{ \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y\right \}, \] and we want to maximise \(\ell(\hat{\mathbf B}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})\) over symmetric positive definite matrices \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\). So take \(\mathbf A=\mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y\). Then, using Proposition ??, \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}} = n^{-1}\mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y\), which agrees with (1.11).

To prove (1.12), note that \[\begin{align*} \text{tr}\{(\mathbf Y-\mathbf X\hat{\mathbf B}) \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}^{-1}(\mathbf Y-\mathbf X\hat{\mathbf B})^\top \} &=\text{tr}\{\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}^{-1} (\mathbf P\mathbf Y)^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y\}\\ &= n \text{tr}\{(\mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y)^{-1} \mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y\}\\ &=n\text{tr}(\mathbf I_p)=np, \end{align*}\] so (1.12) follows after substitution of \(\mathbf B=\hat{\mathbf B}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}=\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}\) into (1.9).

We now determine the joint distribution of \(\hat{\mathbf B}\) and \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}\).

Proposition 1.2 Assume that (1.7) and (1.8) both hold and assume that \(\mathbf X\) has full rank \(q\), where \(q < n\). Then the following results hold.

  1. The MLE of \(\mathbf B\), \(\hat{\mathbf B}\) defined in (1.10), and the MLE of \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\), \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}\) defined in (1.11), are independent. Moreover, the elements of \(\hat{\mathbf B}\) are jointly multivariate normal, while \(n\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}} \sim W_p(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}, n-q)\).

  2. The MLE \(\hat{\mathbf B}\) satisfies \(E[\hat{\mathbf B}]=\mathbf B\), i.e. \(\hat{\mathbf B}\) is unbiased for \(\mathbf B\).

3.Write \(\hat{\mathbf b}_{[j]}\) for column \(j\) of \(\hat{\mathbf B}\), \(j=1, \ldots , p\), so that \(\hat{\mathbf B}=[\hat{\mathbf b}_{[1]}, \ldots , \hat{\mathbf b}_{[p]}]\). Then for \(j,k=1, \ldots , p\), \[ \text{Cov}(\hat{\mathbf b}_{[j]}, \hat{\mathbf b}_{[k]})=\sigma_{jk} (\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}. \]

Proof. of Proposition 1.2 First, observe that \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}\) is a function of the elements of \(\mathbf P\mathbf Y\), namely \[ \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}=n^{-1} \mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y=n^{-1}\mathbf Y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf Y, \] because \(\mathbf P\) is a projection matrix. Moreover, under the MVN assumption (1.8), \(\mathbf P\mathbf Y\) and \(\hat{\mathbf B}\) are jointly MVN, so to prove part 1. it will be sufficient to prove that each column of \(\hat{\mathbf B}\) is uncorrelated with each column of \(\mathbf P\mathbf Y\). Write \(\mathbf y_{[j]}\) for column \(j\) of \(\mathbf Y\). Then \[ \mathbf Y=[\mathbf y_{[1]}, \ldots , \mathbf y_{[p]}], \qquad \mathbf P\mathbf Y=[\mathbf P\mathbf y_{[1]}, \ldots , \mathbf P\mathbf y_{[p]}], \] and \[ \hat{\mathbf B}\equiv [\hat{\mathbf b}_{[1]}, \ldots , \hat{\mathbf b}_{[p]}] =[(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf y_{[1]}, \ldots , (\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf y_{[p]}], \] where, as before, \(\hat{\mathbf b}_{[j]}\) is column \(j\) of \(\hat{\mathbf B}\). The covariance between column \(j\) of \(\hat{\mathbf B}\) and column \(k\) of \(\mathbf P\mathbf Y\) is given by \[\begin{align*} \text{Cov}(\hat{\mathbf b}_{[j]}, \mathbf P\mathbf y_{[k]})&= \text{Cov}\{(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf y_{[j]}, \mathbf P\mathbf y_{[k]}\}\\ &=(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1} \mathbf X^\top \text{Cov}(\mathbf y_{[j]}, \mathbf y_{[k]})\mathbf P\\ &=(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1} \mathbf X^\top \sigma_{jk}\mathbf I_n\mathbf P\\ &=\sigma_{jk}(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1} \mathbf X^\top (\mathbf I_n - \mathbf X(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top)={\mathbf 0}_{q,n}, \end{align*}\] where \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}=(\sigma_{jk})_{j,k=1}^p\). In the above we have used the definition of \(\mathbf P\) in (1.5) and the fact that \[\begin{equation} \text{Cov}(\mathbf y_{[j]}, \mathbf y_{[k]})=\sigma_{jk}\mathbf I_n \tag{1.17} \end{equation}\] due to the independence of the rows of \(\mathbf Y\). Therefore \(\hat{\mathbf B}\) and \(\mathbf P\mathbf Y\) are independent by Proposition ??, and by Proposition ?? (Cochran’s theorem), \(n\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}} \sim W_p(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}, n-q)\) which concludes the proof of part 1.

Part 2. follows because, taking the expectation of (1.10) we find that \[\begin{align*} E[\hat{\mathbf B}]&=E[(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf Y]\\ &=(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top E[\mathbf Y]\\ &=(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X\mathbf B=\mathbf B. \end{align*}\]

For part 3., using (1.17) again, \[\begin{align*} \text{Cov}(\hat{\mathbf b}_{[j]}, \hat{\mathbf b}_{[k]})&=\text{Cov}\{(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf y_{[j]}, (\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1} \mathbf X^\top \mathbf y_{[k]}\}\\ &=(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \text{Cov}(\mathbf y_{[j]}, \mathbf y_{[k]}) \mathbf X(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\\ &=(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1} \mathbf X^\top \sigma_{jk}\mathbf I_n\mathbf X(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}=\sigma_{jk}(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}, \end{align*}\] as required.

We now investigate the situation where we wish to test (1.7) against a sub-model. Specifically, we consider the null hypothesis \[ H_0: \, \mathbf B=\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf B^\ast \\{\mathbf 0}_{q-r,p} \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\quad \text{unrestricted}, \] where \(\mathbf B^\ast\) is \(r \times p\) with \(1 \leq r <q\). The alternative hypothesis is \[ H_1: \, \stackrel{q \times p}{\mathbf B} \quad \text{unrestricted}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\quad \text{unrestricted}, \] i.e. model (1.7) under the MVN assumption (1.8). Note that \(H_0\) is nested within \(H_1\). Let us write \(\hat{\mathbf B}_j\) and \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_j\) for the MLEs of \(\mathbf B\) and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) under hypothesis \(H_j\), \(j=0,1\). Then, using (1.12) in Proposition 1.1 to calculate the maximised likelihood under \(H_0\) and \(H_1\), we obtain the Wilks statistic, \(\omega_{0,1}\) (equals ``twice the different in maximised log likelihoods’’): \[\begin{align*} \omega_{0,1}&=2\{\ell(\hat{\mathbf B}_1, \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_1)-\ell(\hat{\mathbf B}_0, \hat{\Sigma}_0)\}\\ &= - n\log(\vert \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_1\vert) + n\log (\vert \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_0\vert) =n\log \left ( \frac{\vert \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_0 \vert}{\vert \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_1\vert} \right). \end{align*}\]

In the univariate case there is a simple, explicit transformation from the Wilks statistic \(\omega_{0,1}\) to the classical \(F_{q-r,n-q}\) distribution for testing \(H_0\) against \(H_1\). In the multivariate case, the exact distribution of \(\omega_{0,1}\) under \(H_0\) does not have a simple relationship with an \(F\)-distribution, and the situation is a lot more complicated.

When \(n\) is large, however, we can use the large sample log-likelihood ratio test, which implies that, under \(H_0\), \(\omega_{0,1}\) is approximately \(\chi^2\), and we should reject \(H_0\) when \(\omega_{0,1}\) is sufficiently large.

The relevant degrees of freedom of the \(\chi^2\) are now calculated.

Under \(H_0\), the number of free parameters is \[ rp + \frac{1}{2}p(p+1), \] while under \(H_1\) there are \[ qp +\frac{1}{2}p(p+1) \] free parameters. So the difference is \((q-r)p\), and so we should refer \(\omega_{0,1}\) to \(\chi_{(q-r)p}^2\).

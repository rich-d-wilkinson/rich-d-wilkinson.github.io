2.7.1 Lines and Hyperplanes in \(\mathbb{R}^p\)

For any \(\mathbf a, \mathbf b\in \mathbb{R}^p\), the set \[\begin{equation} \mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}(\mathbf a, \mathbf b)=\{\mathbf a+\gamma \mathbf b: \gamma \in \mathbb{R}\} \tag{2.2} \end{equation}\] is a straight line in \(\mathbb{R}^p\).

If \(\mathbf a^\top \mathbf b=0\), i.e. \(\mathbf a\) and \(\mathbf b\) are orthogonal, then \(\mathbf a\) is the perpendicular from the origin \({\mathbf 0}_p\) to the line \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf a,\mathbf b).\)

For fixed \(\mathbf a\in \mathbb{R}^p\) and \(\gamma \in \mathbb{R}\), \[ \mathcal{H}=\mathcal{H}(\mathbf a, \gamma) =\{\mathbf x\in \mathbb{R}^p:\, \mathbf a^\top \mathbf x=\gamma\} \] is a hyperplane of dimension \(p-1\) in \(\mathbb{R}^p\). There is an alternative way to define hyperplanes in \(\mathbb{R}^P\). Suppose that, for \(1 \leq r <p\), \(\stackrel{p \times 1}{\mathbf a}_1, \ldots , \stackrel{p \times 1}{\mathbf a}_r, \stackrel{p \times 1}{\mathbf a}_{r+1}\) are linearly independent. Then \[ \mathcal{H}=\left \{ \sum_{j=1}^{r+1} \gamma_j \mathbf a_j: \, \sum_{j=1}^{r+1}\gamma_j =1 \right \} \] is an \(r\)-dimensional hyperplane in \(\mathbb{R}^p\).

When \(r=1\), using the fact that \(\gamma_1+\gamma_2=1\), we may write \[ \gamma_1 \mathbf a_1 + \gamma_2 \mathbf a_2=(1-\gamma_2)\mathbf a_1 + \gamma_2 \mathbf a_2 = \mathbf a_1 +\gamma_2(\mathbf a_2-\mathbf a_1), \] which agrees with \(\mathbf a+\gamma \mathbf b\) in (2.2) when \(\mathbf a= \mathbf a_1\), \(\mathbf b= \mathbf a_2 -\mathbf a_1\) and \(\gamma=\gamma_2\). So we have shown that the two definitions agree in the case of a straight line.

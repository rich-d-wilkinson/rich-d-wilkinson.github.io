Multivariate Statistics

1.1 Recap: standard linear model

This section contains a brief review of the standard linear model in which the response is univariate. Most of you will have studied linear models in other modules.

We assume that:

  1. For each \(i=1, \ldots , n\), \({\mathbb{E}}[\epsilon_i]=0\).
  2. The \(\epsilon_i\) are uncorrelated, i.e. for \(i \neq j\), \({\mathbb{C}\operatorname{ov}}(\epsilon_i, \epsilon_j)=0\).
  3. The \(\epsilon_i\) have constant variance (i.e., are homoscedastic), i.e. \({\mathbb{V}\operatorname{ar}}(\epsilon_i)=\sigma^2\), i.e. \(\sigma^2\) does not depend on \(i\).

One way to choose \(\boldsymbol \beta\) is to choose it to minimize the sum of squared errors, i.e., \[\hat{\boldsymbol \beta} = \arg \min_\boldsymbol \beta|| \mathbf y- \mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta||_2^2.\]

If we note that \[|| \mathbf y- \mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta||_2^2 = (\mathbf y-\mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta)^\top(\mathbf y-\mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta) \] then multiplying out and differentiating with respect to \(\boldsymbol \beta\) gives \[\frac{\mathrm d}{\mathrm d\boldsymbol \beta} || \mathbf y- \mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta||_2^2 = \mathbf X^\top (\mathbf y- \mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta).\] You may need to look again at ?? to remind yourself about vector differentiation. Setting the derivative equal to \(\boldsymbol 0\) and solving gives the least squares estimator

\[\begin{equation} \hat{\boldsymbol \beta}=\left (\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X\right )^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf y \tag{1.3} \end{equation}\] when \(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X\) is invertible (we’ll discuss what to do if it is not in the next section). The fitted-values are \[\hat{\mathbf y}= \mathbf X\hat{\boldsymbol \beta}=\mathbf X(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf y.\]

1.1.1 Geometry

Does this make sense intuitively? As discussed in Chapter ??, we know that \(\mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta\) is a linear combination of the columns of \(\mathbf X\) and is thus in \(\mathcal{C}(\mathbf X),\) the columns space of \(\mathbf X\). Thus minimizing \(||\mathbf y- \mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta||_2^2\) is equivalent to finding the orthogonal projection of \(\mathbf y\) onto \(\mathcal{C}(\mathbf X)\).

\[\mathbf P_{\mathcal{C}(X)}\mathbf y=\arg \min_{\mathbf y': \mathbf y' \in \mathcal{C}(X)} ||\mathbf y-\mathbf y'||_2.\] and by Proposition ?? this is \[\mathbf P_{\mathcal{C}(X)}\mathbf y= \mathbf X(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf y=\hat{\mathbf y}\] which again implies
\[\hat{\boldsymbol \beta}=(\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \mathbf y\] in agreement with what we found in the previous section. So we can think of linear regression as projecting \(\mathbf y\) onto the columns of \(\mathbf X\).

1.1.2 Normal linear model

Often, we make an additional (fourth) assumption about the random errors and assume that they are normally distributed:

  1. The \(\epsilon_i\) are IID \(N(0, \sigma^2)\).

The log-likelihood for models (1.1) and (1.2) under the Gaussian assumption 4. is \[\begin{align*} \ell(\boldsymbol \beta, \sigma^2)&=-\frac{n}{2}\log (\sigma^2)-\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi)-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i-\mathbf x_i^\top \boldsymbol \beta)^2\\ & = -\frac{n}{2}\log (\sigma^2)-\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi)-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} (\mathbf y- \mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta)^\top (\mathbf y- \mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta). \end{align*}\] We can see that maximizing the log-likelihood with respect to \(\boldsymbol \beta\) is equivalent to minimizing \(||\mathbf y- \mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta||_2^2=(\mathbf y- \mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta)^\top (\mathbf y- \mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta)\) and thus \(\hat{\boldsymbol \beta}\) is also the maximum likelihood estimator of \(\boldsymbol \beta\) when the errors have a Gaussian distribution.

In addition \[ \frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \sigma^2}(\boldsymbol \beta, \sigma^2)=-\frac{n}{2}\frac{1}{\sigma^2}+\frac{1}{2\sigma^4} (\mathbf y- \mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta)^\top (\mathbf y- \mathbf X\boldsymbol \beta). \] and setting \(\partial \ell(\hat{\boldsymbol \beta}, \hat{\sigma}^2))/\partial \sigma^2=0\) gives \[\begin{equation} \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\mathbf y^\top \mathbf P\mathbf y, \tag{1.4} \end{equation}\] where \[\begin{equation} \mathbf P=\mathbf I_n - \mathbf X\left ( \mathbf X^\top \mathbf X\right)^{-1}\mathbf X^\top \tag{1.5} \end{equation}\] is a projection matrix.
Under the IID Gaussian assumption for the \(\epsilon_i\), \[ \hat{\boldsymbol \beta} \sim N_p\left \{\boldsymbol \beta, \sigma^2 (\mathbf X^\top \mathbf X)^{-1}\right\}, \] and \[ n\hat{\sigma}^2/\sigma^2 \sim \chi_{n-p}^2 \] which allows us to do hypothesis tests about the value of \(\boldsymbol \beta\) (but we do not pursue that in this module).

1.1.3 Linear models in R

Linear models are easy to fit in R. For example, using the iris data, we can train a model to predict the sepal length from the other measurements as follows:

lm(Sepal.Length~ Sepal.Width+Petal.Length+Petal.Width,data=iris)

## lm(formula = Sepal.Length ~ Sepal.Width + Petal.Length + Petal.Width, 
##     data = iris)
##

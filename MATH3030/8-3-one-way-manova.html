8.3 One-way MANOVA

We now consider the multivariate version of the one-way ANOVA considered at the end of 8.2, known as one-way MANOVA. The model is defined by \[\begin{equation} \mathbf y_{ij} = {\boldsymbol{\mu}}_j + {\pmb \epsilon}_{ij}, \qquad i=1, \ldots , n_j; \quad j=1, \ldots, t, \tag{8.21} \end{equation}\] where the \({\pmb \epsilon}_{ij}\) are IID \(N_p({\mathbf 0}_p, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})\), and the \(\mathbf y_{ij}\) and \({\boldsymbol{\mu}}_j\) are also \(p \times 1\) vectors. It is assumed that \(\text{Var}({\pmb \epsilon}_i)=\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) is constant across the \(t\) populations.

One possibility is to use the linear model framework developed in 8.3. In this case, \(\mathbf X\) is a matrix with each element equal to zero or one. However, it is also feasible to do the calculations directly. This is what we shall do.

The log-likelihood of model (8.21) is given by \[\begin{align} \ell({\boldsymbol{\mu}}_1, \ldots , {\boldsymbol{\mu}}_t)&=-\frac{np}{2}\log(2\pi)-\frac{n}{2}\log(\vert \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\vert)\nonumber\\ & \qquad \qquad -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^t \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} (\mathbf y_{ij}-{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_j)^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf y_{ij}-{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_j), \tag{8.22} \end{align}\] where \(n=n_1 + n_2 +\cdots + n_t\).

Using results in 2.10, the partial derivative, or gradient, of (8.22) with respect to the vector \({\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k\) is given by \[\begin{align*} \frac{\partial \ell}{\partial {\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k}({\boldsymbol{\mu}}_1, \ldots , {\boldsymbol{\mu}}_t, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})&=\sum_{i=1}^{n_j} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\mathbf y_{ik}-{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k)\\ &=n_k \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\bar{\mathbf y}_{+k}-{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k). \end{align*}\] where \(\bar{\mathbf y}_{+k}\) is the sample mean of group \(k\), i.e. \[ \bar{\mathbf y}_{+k}=n_k^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n_k} \mathbf y_{ik}. \] So, setting \(\partial \ell/\partial {\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k={\mathbf 0}_p\) implies \(\hat{{\boldsymbol{\mu}}}_k=\bar{\mathbf y}_{+k}\). Therefore \[\begin{align*} \ell(\hat{{\boldsymbol{\mu}}}_1, \ldots , \hat{{\boldsymbol{\mu}}}_t, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})&= -\frac{np}{2}\log(2\pi)-\frac{n}{2}\log(\vert \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\vert)\\ &\qquad \qquad - \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^t \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} (\mathbf y_{ij}-\bar{\mathbf y}_{+j})^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf y_{ij}-\bar{\mathbf y}_{+j})\\ &=-\frac{n p}{2}\log(2\pi) -\frac{n}{2}\log(\vert \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\vert)-\frac{1}{2}\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf W), \end{align*}\] where \(\mathbf W\), defined by \[ \mathbf W=\sum_{j=1}^t \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} (\mathbf y_{ij}-\bar{\mathbf y}_{+j})(\mathbf y_{ij}-\bar{\mathbf y}_{+j})^\top, \] is the matrix version of the `within’ sum of squares considered in 8.2. Using Proposition ??, we deduce that \[ \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}=n^{-1}\mathbf W. \] Consequently, \[ \ell(\hat{{\boldsymbol{\mu}}}_1, \ldots , \hat{{\boldsymbol{\mu}}}_t,\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}})=-\frac{n}{2}\log(\vert \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}\vert)-\frac{np}{2}- \frac{np}{2}\log(2\pi). \]

Now consider the key null hypothesis for a one-way MANOVA: \[ H_0: \, {\boldsymbol{\mu}}_1=\cdots ={\boldsymbol{\mu}}_t, \qquad \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\quad \text{unrestricted}. \] Under \(H_0\), the \(\mathbf y_{ij}\) are IID \(N_p({\boldsymbol{\mu}}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})\), so the log-likelihood under \(H_0\) is \[\begin{align*} \ell_0({\boldsymbol{\mu}}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}) & \equiv \ell ({\boldsymbol{\mu}}, \ldots ,{\boldsymbol{\mu}}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})\\ &=-\frac{np}{2}\log(2\pi) - \frac{n}{2}\log(\vert \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\vert)-\frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^t \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} (\mathbf y_{ij}-{\boldsymbol{\mu}})^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\mathbf y_{ij}-{\boldsymbol{\mu}}), \end{align*}\] and so the MLE of \({\boldsymbol{\mu}}\) under \(H_0\) is given by \[ \hat{{\boldsymbol{\mu}}}_0=\frac{1}{n} \sum_{j=1}^t \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} \mathbf y_{ij} =\bar{\mathbf y}_{++}, \] and, using Proposition ?? again, it is seen that the MLE of \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) under \(H_0\) is \[ \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_0=n^{-1}\mathbf T, \] where \(\mathbf T\) is the matrix analogue of the total sum of squares, i.e. \[ \mathbf T=\sum_{j=1}^t \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} (\mathbf y_{ij}-\bar{\mathbf y}_{++})(\mathbf y_{ij}-\bar{\mathbf y}_{++})^\top. \]

The Wilks statistic, \(\omega_0\), for testing \(H_0\) against the general alternative (8.21) is then \[\begin{align} \omega_0=2\{\ell(\hat{{\boldsymbol{\mu}}}_1, \ldots , \hat{{\boldsymbol{\mu}}}_t, \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}})-\ell_0(\hat{{\boldsymbol{\mu}}}_0, \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_0)\}&=n\log \left (\vert \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_0 \vert / \vert \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}\vert \right ) \nonumber \\ &=n \log\left ( \vert \mathbf T\vert/\vert \mathbf W\vert \right ). \tag{8.23} \end{align}\] The degrees of freedom under \(H_0\) are \(p+p(p+1)/2\) and the degrees of freedom under (8.21) are \(pt+p(p+1)/2\), so the difference is \(p(t-1)\). Consequently, when the \(n_j\) are all large, we should refer \(\omega_0\) to \(\chi_{p(t-1)}^2\) and reject \(H_0\) when \(\omega_0\) is sufficiently large.

It is not immediately obvious that (8.23) is a natural generalisation of (8.8). However, in Example Sheet 4 you are asked to prove that \[ \mathbf T=\mathbf W+\mathbf B, \] where \(\mathbf B\) is the matrix analogue of the `between’ sum of squares \(B\) in @ref(eq:T=B+W), i.e. \[ \mathbf B= \sum_{j=1}^t n_j (\bar{\mathbf y}_{+j}-\bar{\mathbf y}_{++})(\bar{\mathbf y}_{+j}-\bar{\mathbf y}_{++})^\top. \] Consequently, \[\begin{align*} \omega_0&=n \log (\vert \mathbf T\vert /\vert \mathbf W\vert) =n \log (\vert \mathbf W^{-1} \vert \vert \mathbf W+ \mathbf B\vert)\\ &=n \log (\vert \mathbf W^{-1}(\mathbf W+\mathbf B)\vert) =n \log (\vert \mathbf I_p +\mathbf W^{-1} \mathbf B\vert), \end{align*}\] which a natural generalisation of (8.8).
